What Is Quizbowl?

Part V: The Distribution

There is an important question that follows from this explanation so far: what subjects do quizbowl questions focus on? There is no definite answer to the question, but the quizbowl community agrees that the topics in a question set should remain roughly constant from round to round. If there are four history tossups in Round 1, then there should be four history tossups in Round 2. Even though it is impossible to have exactly the same topics in each round, the goal is to have as little variation as possible. The precise number of questions per topic in each round is called the "distribution".

Distributions vary from question set to question set, but there are some conventions that most question sets follow. It is common for question sets to have the majority of questions in a round come from one of the "Big Three" categories: History, Literature, and Science + Math. The remaining questions are then pulled from secondary categories, which are usually encompassed by Fine Arts (painting, classical music, other art), Beliefs + Thought (religion, mythology, philosophy, social science), and Modern World + Other (geography, current events, popular culture, miscellaneous). However, this is only the case for standard academic tournaments. There are tournaments on just pop culture (as well as other non-standard topics) with wildly varying distributions. Ultimately, the exact distribution just depends on the opinions of the editors and writers.

Most question sets also try to standardize the number of questions that come from each subcategory within these broader categories. For example, the PACE National Scholastic Championship has 4 history tossups and history bonuses per 20 question round, and those 4 history questions always contain 1 American question, 2 European questions, and 1 World question. The result is a game that is predictable; without the variation present in trivia, it becomes far more straightforward for players to improve.

Question sets are supposed to outline their exact distribution beforehand so players know what to expect. Each topic is assigned two numbers separated by a slash, which indicates the number of tossups and the number of bonuses in a round. For example, 5/4 Literature means that there are 5 Literature tossups per round and 4 literature bonuses per round. 1.5/1.5 American History means that there are 3 American History tossups and 3 American History bonuses every 2 rounds. Some of the most well-known question set providers are NAQT, ACF, and PACE, each of which have their own distribution. You can find these organizations' full distributions by clicking on their names.

Pyramidality and an intentional subject distribution are two of the most important ingredients in a high quality quizbowl question set. But these are not the only ingredients, as we shall see in Part VI: Question Anatomy.
